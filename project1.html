You Get What You Pay For

A Story About "Hiring" High School Kids

Back when I attended high school I took web development courses, all the way up to Web Coding Three. I unfortunately only remember enough at this point to know I used to be better at web coding, but even when I did "know" what I was doing, I was still just a high school kid.

Enter: Opportunistic Entrepreneur

Our teacher for Web Three had the idea of having us construct an actual website for a real person, and managed to find someone who was starting a massage business. After figuring out what the client generally wanted for their website, I was chosen as the representative (among the students in the class) to communicate with the client via email whenever we needed files, feedback, clarification, etc. This also meant that the client would contact me whenever they had an idea or wanted something, which would come back to bite me.

The Dumpster Fire

As it turns out, this client already had some of a website made from a service that makes templated websites, think SquareSpace. Naturally when our client figured out that they could replace the service they were paying for with free labor from the local high school, they jumped on it. That led to me and the other students parsing through this nightmare generated code for about a week before deciding that starting completely from scratch would be better than trying to work with this Frankenstein abomination.

More Than I Could Chew

Big surprise, but making a website for someone trying to promote a business can be a full time job. On top of having other classes and an actual job, I was drowning in work to get done. I remember staying up for a couple of nights to get a logo photoshopped together for the client because it was the only time I could find to work on it. To make matter worse, my other "co-workers" were also high school students, so I would frequently have to let our client know why certain things weren't done when they said they would be and take the brunt of the heat for it.

End Of The Road

By the end of the class I was fed up with coding. Between being customer service at a grocery store and customer service for our client it was too much. I hated my classmates who constantly came up short on deadlines and left it to me to either buy more time or do it myself. I decided coding wasn't the path for me, so much so that it took me years before I entertained the idea of tyring it again for an advanced degree, and while web coding still leaves me with a bad taste, I've come around on the idea of programming in other languages.
